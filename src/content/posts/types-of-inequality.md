title: Types of inequality

Inequality is a social choice, and a bad one. This is a field guide to its forms — emergent and adversarial — and the mechanisms that produce them.

## Emergent inequality

Emergent inequality arises from agents following rules, where no single rule is unfair and yet the result is unequal. Nobody cheated. Nobody planned it. Accountability is diffuse — there is no person, decision, or moment to point at. It is a property of the system. Identical agents, symmetric rules, outcome: oligarchy. This does not mean nobody is responsible. It means the remedy is design, not punishment. The only corrective is a deliberate restoring force at the social layer.

### Anti-egalitarian games

**Yard-sale model.** Two people bet a fraction of the poorer person's wealth on a fair coin flip, over and over. Total wealth never changes; one person ends up with nearly everything. The same bet means different things to different people. Losing ten dollars when you have ten is ruin. Losing ten when you have ten thousand is nothing. Fair in absolute terms, brutal in relative ones.

**Multiplicative growth trap.** Everyone invests; wealth grows or shrinks by a random percentage each year. The average return is positive, but almost every individual goes broke. A 50% loss followed by a 50% gain leaves you at 75%. Losses hit harder than equivalent gains. The population looks healthy; the individuals do not. This is broken ergodicity — the average across people and the average across time for one person come apart.

**Pólya urn.** An urn holds one red and one blue ball. Draw one, return it, add another of the same colour. Whichever is drawn first gains a compounding edge. The process locks into an arbitrary ratio decided by the first few draws. Path dependence: early accidents become permanent structure. Brian Arthur used urn-like models to explain technology lock-in — VHS over Betamax, QWERTY over Dvorak.

**Minority game.** An odd number of people pick one of two doors; only the minority get paid. The only equilibrium is to choose at random, and then cumulative winnings drift apart like random walks. No strategy makes everyone equal. The structure shows up in financial markets, where contrarian traders face it directly.

**Preferential attachment.** People arrive one at a time and form connections proportional to those others already have. Early arrivals become hubs, not because they are better but because they were there first. Power-law distributions show up in the web, citation networks, and follower counts.

**Bonabeau hierarchy.** Identical agents fight at random. Winners get a confidence boost, losers a penalty. Future fights are biased by confidence, and a strict hierarchy emerges from perfect equality. Crayfish implement it biologically: early winners get a serotonin lift that makes them more aggressive next time.

### Feedback loops

A small advantage compounds. Nobody cheats. The mechanism just amplifies whatever difference already exists.

**Compound interest.** Two people, same income. One inherits £10,000 at 20, the other nothing. At 60, at 5%, the first has £70,000 from that alone. The gap came from one event forty years earlier. Money makes money.

**Early literacy.** A child who reads slightly better at five gets more praise, more books, more confidence, reads more. The slightly worse reader gets less. By ten the gap is enormous. Weeks of brain development become years of divergence — the Matthew effect.

**Playground dynamics.** Within minutes, some children lead and others follow. The leaders arrived first, spoke loudest, suggested the first game. The hierarchy forms before anyone has demonstrated competence. Bonabeau at six years old.

**Accent of confidence.** People praised early speak with more certainty. Certainty reads as competence. They get promoted. The promotion confirms the certainty. The original praise may have been random.

**Platform monopolies.** More users attract more sellers, which attract more users. Now there is one Amazon, one Google, one app store. Nobody mandated a monopoly; the feedback loop produced it.

**Attention concentration.** Wherever visibility compounds — social media, publishing, art — a small early audience snowballs. Most creators are invisible not because they are worse but because nobody saw them first.

**Health–wealth spirals.** Poor health lowers productivity, lowers income, worsens health. No single actor causes the spiral, but it produces persistent inequality between people with identical starting potential.

**Funding in science.** An early grant leads to papers, which lead to more grants, more students, more citations. Miss the early grant and you fall behind permanently. The science that gets done is the science that got funded first.

**Institutional survivor bias.** People who succeed inside an institution are the people the institution was already designed to favour. They rise, set policy, hire people like themselves. The institution becomes more like itself over time. Not conspiracy — a filter that compounds.

**First job, second job.** Your first job shapes your second. Two equally capable people in different first jobs diverge rapidly — networks, skills, reputation. The CV is a Pólya urn.

**Beauty standards.** Features overrepresented among the powerful become markers of attractiveness. Media amplifies them; industries form around them; people internalise them. Nobody designed the standard. It emerged from who held power when mass media arrived.

**Credential cascades.** Once some jobs require degrees, others start to. The work hasn't changed; employers use credentials as a filter. The barrier escalates with nobody raising it deliberately.

**Norm cascades.** A behaviour becomes common, then expected, then deviating becomes costly. Sixty-hour weeks were not mandated, but once enough people work them, not doing so marks you as uncommitted.

**Care work.** Someone must look after children, the sick, the old, and that work is unpaid or underpaid. Whoever does it falls behind economically. Expectations compound, infrastructure assumes a carer at home, policy follows the assumption. The gender wealth gap is what comes out the other side.

**Algorithmic feedback.** A recommender shows you what people like you clicked on. You click. It shows more. Multiply across millions and you get polarisation, filter bubbles, radicalisation. Nobody programmed it to polarise; it optimised for engagement and polarisation emerged.

### Path dependence

Early accidents become permanent structure. The outcome could have gone the other way, but once it happened it locked in.

**Postcode.** Where your parents live determines your school, peers, accent, network, safety, air quality. None of it is chosen. A postcode is a lottery that compounds across a lifetime.

**Naming.** Identical CVs with different names get different callback rates. A name signals ethnicity, class, sometimes religion. Your parents chose it from within their social layer. It precedes you into every room.

**Network hiring.** Most jobs are filled through personal connections. Your network is inherited from your parents, your school, your neighbourhood. The gate is the network itself.

**Standards and conventions.** English is the global language not because it is better but because of path dependence. A Thai scientist who must publish in English to be taken seriously is experiencing emergent inequality.

**Queue position in history.** Europeans colonised rather than being colonised partly because of geography — Eurasia's east-west axis let crops and livestock spread across similar climates, speeding agriculture. The material head start compounded socially into empires. The colonised were not inferior. They were late in a queue they did not know they were in.

**Noise as destiny.** Every evaluation — exam, interview, review — has measurement error. Under positive feedback, a lucky early evaluation compounds into a real advantage. Your career was shaped by whether the interviewer had a good lunch.

### Structural sorting

The built environment and material conditions filter people. Nobody designed the filter as a filter; it emerged from economics, geography, and accumulated decisions.

**Spatial sorting.** Cities grow where rivers meet, ports form, wealth concentrates, talent migrates toward it, the wealth compounds. Nobody decided London should dominate the UK economy. It emerged.

**Accent and dialect.** Some accents read as authority and intelligence, others as ignorance. Nobody decided RP sounds smart — it emerged from who held power when broadcasting started. Speakers of stigmatised accents code-switch, which is labour the "standard" speakers never perform.

**Dress codes.** Knowing how to dress for an interview, a dinner, a court appearance is socially transmitted. Show up in the wrong clothes and you are filtered out before you speak. Nobody wrote the rule. The dress code does it automatically.

**Informational asymmetry.** A banker's child knows about interest rates; a farmer's child knows about crops. Knowledge is inherited socially and compounds through access to more knowledge. Networks are unequal.

**Urban heat islands.** Poor neighbourhoods have fewer trees, more concrete, less green space. The temperature difference between neighbourhoods in the same city can be 5–10°C. Poor people are literally hotter, sicker, and die earlier in heatwaves.

**Traffic and lungs.** Major roads get routed through poor neighbourhoods because land is cheaper and resistance is weaker. The children who grow up beside them have higher rates of asthma. The routing was economic. The asthma is emergent.

**Shift work.** The economy runs 24 hours; someone works nights. Night work damages health, shortens life, disrupts family. Who does it? The people with the least bargaining power.

**Dental health.** Childhood access to a dentist determines your teeth for life, and your teeth shape how people read trustworthiness, intelligence, employability. The inequality is visible, permanent, and nobody's individual fault.

### Information cascades

Everyone watches what everyone else does and copies. The initial signal may be wrong, but it becomes self-fulfilling. Distinct from feedback loops because the mechanism is copying under uncertainty, not compounding advantage.

**Restaurant queues.** A queue signals quality, so the queue attracts more people. The empty restaurant next door may be just as good. The first few customers chose at random; everyone after them chose based on that.

**Bank runs.** A solvent bank. A rumour. A few withdrawals. Others see them and withdraw too. The bank runs out of cash — not because the rumour was true, but because enough people acted as if it was.

**Fashion and trends.** A few visible people wear something. Others copy. The thing becomes a trend, then a norm. The cascade manufactures a consensus that nobody individually held.

### Threshold effects

Some inequalities are stable until a critical point, then collapse suddenly. The system looks fine until it isn't. Invisible until irreversible.

**Gentrification.** A few wealthier people move in. Rents rise. A coffee shop opens. More wealthier people arrive. At some point the original residents can no longer afford to stay. Slow, then sudden.

**Language death.** A minority language loses speakers gradually. People still speak it at home — until the count drops below the threshold where children stop learning it, because there aren't enough other children to speak it with. The decline is linear; the death is a cliff.

**Ecological collapse.** A fishery is overfished gradually. Yields decline but the system holds. Then the population drops below the point where it can reproduce faster than it's caught. Centuries of stock, gone in a decade.

### Asymmetric risk exposure

The same event hits everyone, but the damage is distributed unequally. The risk is shared; the consequences are not. The shock is symmetric; the stack filtering it is not.

**Recessions.** A recession hits the whole economy. It destroys the poor and inconveniences the rich. A worker loses their job, home, and health insurance. An investor waits a few years for the portfolio to recover.

**Pandemics.** A virus doesn't know who you are. But who dies depends on who can work from home, who has savings, who has healthcare, who lives in crowded housing. The essential workers stacked shelves and drove buses and died. The remote workers were bored.

**Climate change.** The atmosphere warms for everyone, but the people who contributed least to emissions — subsistence farmers in the Global South — bear the worst consequences. The physics is global. The damage is local, and it falls along pre-existing lines of inequality.

## Adversarial inequality

Adversarial inequality is created or maintained by agents who benefit from it. Someone is at fault. Most real inequality is both: the initial divergence is emergent, but once someone is ahead they have the means and the motive to keep it that way — funding think tanks, capturing regulators, building material lock-in. The emergence may be blameless. The maintenance rarely is.

### Extraction

Taking value without creating it. Sitting between others and collecting a cut.

**Rent-seeking.** Using position to extract value without producing any. Landlords who raise rents because the neighbourhood improved. Patent trolls who sue rather than build. Monopolists who raise prices because they can.

**Land banking.** Buying land and holding it empty, waiting for value to rise. The community develops the area; the landowner captures the gain without contributing.

**Academic publishing.** Researchers do the work, publicly funded. They give the paper to a journal for free. Reviewers work for free. The journal charges universities thousands for access. The publisher adds nothing.

**Tipping culture.** Shifting the cost of labour from employer to customer. The employer pays below minimum wage; the worker depends on the goodwill of strangers; the customer carries the moral burden. The employer pockets the difference.

**Privatisation of public goods.** Selling off utilities, transport, housing built with public money, then letting the new owner extract rent. The public paid for the asset. The private owner collects.

### Capture

Getting control of the rules. The people the rules are meant to constrain end up writing them.

**Regulatory capture.** Banks lobbying to weaken capital requirements. Fossil fuel companies on climate advisory boards. Pharma influencing drug approval. The referee works for one of the teams.

**Lobbying asymmetry.** The fossil fuel industry spends billions. The citizens who breathe the air spend nothing. One side has professional advocates; the other has day jobs.

**Gerrymandering.** Drawing electoral boundaries so your party wins regardless of how people vote. Same voters, different map, predetermined outcome. Democracy is formally intact; power is rigged.

**Tax avoidance.** Not illegal — by design. The wealthy fund the lobbying that creates the loopholes they use. The complexity of the tax code is itself a barrier; you need advisors to game it.

**Shareholder primacy.** The doctrine that a company exists to maximise shareholder value above all else. Not a law of nature — a social-layer choice made in the 1970s and 80s that redirected productivity gains from workers to owners.

### Suppression

Preventing others from gaining power. The direct approach.

**Wage suppression.** Anti-union activity, non-compete clauses, wage-fixing agreements, opposing minimum wage. This is not the market clearing — it is employers coordinating to stop it from clearing.

**Union suppression.** Firing organisers, closing unionised plants, mandatory anti-union meetings, right-to-work laws. The explicit goal is to prevent collective bargaining.

**Non-competes for low-wage workers.** A sandwich shop worker barred from another sandwich shop for a year. No trade secrets to protect — the clause exists to suppress bargaining power.

**Criminalisation of poverty.** Fines for sleeping rough. Arrest for loitering. Bail that imprisons the poor before trial while the wealthy go home. The law punishes the condition, not the conduct.

**NDA culture.** Non-disclosure agreements used to silence employees who experienced harassment or unsafe conditions. The NDA doesn't resolve the problem; it hides it.

### Enclosure

Taking what was shared and making it private.

**Enclosure of commons.** Common land. Water. Publicly funded research behind paywalls. Patented seeds. Taking something from the commons and charging for access to what people used to have.

**IP maximalism.** Copyright extended to life plus seventy. Evergreened pharmaceutical patents. Using IP not to protect innovation but to maintain monopoly rents long after the inventor is dead.

**Credentialism as gatekeeping.** Requiring degrees for jobs that don't need them, knowing degree access correlates with class. Adversarial when the requirement is maintained because it filters for class, not despite it.

### Debt and dependency

Structuring relationships so the weaker party cannot leave.

**Debt as control.** Lending on terms designed to keep the borrower in debt. Payday loans. Student debt that survives bankruptcy. IMF structural adjustment programmes. The creditor doesn't want you to pay it off — the interest is the point.

**Predatory lending.** Targeting people who cannot afford the loan with terms designed to maximise interest before default. Subprime mortgages. 400% APR payday loans.

**Planned obsolescence.** Designing products to break. Phones that slow after updates. Printers that refuse to print when one cartridge is "low." Dependency engineered in.

**Algorithmic pricing.** Charging different prices to different people based on what data suggests they will pay. Uber surge pricing in poor neighbourhoods. Insurance by postcode. Optimised for extraction, not fairness.

### Externalisation

Keeping the profit, exporting the harm.

**Pollution and risk.** Dumping waste in poor communities. Pushing risk onto gig workers. Supply chains that move poverty offshore so the consumer doesn't see it.

**Offshoring harm.** Moving factories to countries with weaker labour and environmental laws. The product is consumed in the wealthy country; the pollution and the unsafe conditions stay in the poor one.

**Transfer pricing.** Booking profits in low-tax jurisdictions where the company has no real operations. Ireland, Luxembourg, the Caymans. The profits are real; the location is fictional; the lost tax revenue would have funded schools.

**Austerity as class war.** Cutting public services in a downturn, when the poor need them most, to balance budgets unbalanced by tax cuts for the wealthy. Cut taxes, borrow to cover the gap, then cut services to pay the debt. The deficit was manufactured.

### Narrative control

Shaping the story so the arrangement seems inevitable.

**Naturalisation weaponised.** Funding think tanks, owning media, shaping curricula. Constructing the story that the current arrangement is natural, deserved, inevitable. "Job creators." "Trickle-down." "Meritocracy."

**Legacy engineering.** Using current advantage to ensure advantage persists across generations. Trust funds, legacy admissions, nepotism, inheritance, private schools that feed elite networks. The goal isn't only to be ahead — it's to make sure your children are too.

**Violence and threat.** The most direct form. Union-busting. Intimidating whistleblowers. Police protecting property over people. Colonial extraction backed by military force. Sometimes the adversary skips the mechanism and just uses force.
